## My Experience

Ever since I started learning to code, I relied on online resources to help me solve my problems. Looking up forum threads like Stack Overflow helped me answer majority of my questions and if I couldn’t find my answer, I would rely on the professor or TA. But I never thought of creating a thread myself to solve a problem. It might be that I was intimidated to post about my ignorance online or that I was aware that there were guidelines set to posting on these forums. However, I wasn’t curious enough in the past to learn about what it takes to post effective, smart questions on the internet. 

Eric Steven Raymond had a very detailed essay regarding the precepts of asking smart questions. It made me contemplate how professional hackers have to deal with questions everyday and I realize that it takes a certain amount of consideration for them if you’re stuck on a problem and want a constructive response back. Many people post on the forums as they’re so easily accessible, but some don’t always get the answer their looking for. Most of the time, only those who can appeal to the professionals can expect a positive response.

## Asking questions, good and bad

 I found that one of the main things to consider when posting a question on a thread is to do your research beforehand. Since hackers can be limited on time and aren’t committed to answering a question, one must convince the expert that they have put the effort in to solving a problem on their own and is willing to learn, not just have answers handed down to them. There is a decent example on Stack Overflow demonstrating someone who may have done their research before asking a forum. The question was “How can I loop through all the entries in an array using JavaScript?” Sure, this could have been answered by the right search, but they also displayed an example of their attempt, showing that they are trying to learn and maybe just a little confused, possibly with converting from different languages. In return, the poster received over 4,000 upvotes, many replies, and a very detailed answer and explanation by the expert who was selected as the best answer. 

In contrast, a person who asks for someone to simply explain something to them is asking a dumb question and in response, probably won’t get much answers. Another post on Stack Overflow has the subject line “Explanation for in loop JavaScript” with the body saying “Who can explain how this for in loop works and why it's assign keys of object to array?” For one, this doesn’t display proper grammar, especially in the subject line which doesn’t attract many hackers. Also, below the body description is the snippet of the persons code, that’s it. There’s no display that the person did their research, where they could easily find a tutorial on this function or a clear description of what there deviation to their expected outcome was. In response, this person has received only one answer for the past two years and comments saying that they should look up a forum. In a sense, this wastes hackers time when the poster didn’t put time into it themselves to answer the question. 

## My Observation

The way you ask questions can greatly impact if and how you get a response. I’ve learned that you must value the time that others give you and put as much effort into something as you want out of it. Following this mindset will not just get you liked by others, but you’ll see the best results. It’s like there’s an algorithm to solving your problem, inside the code and out.
